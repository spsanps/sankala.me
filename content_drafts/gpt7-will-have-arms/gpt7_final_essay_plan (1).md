## THE FORMAT DECISION

### What This Essay IS:
- A **Situational Awareness-style strategic essay** (long-form, rigorous, consequential)
- An **argument piece** with a clear, falsifiable thesis
- A **framework for understanding** unfolding events
- **Readable in one sitting** (~8,000-12,000 words)

### What This Essay IS NOT:
- A timeline prediction document ("In Q3 2026, X will happen...")
- A comprehensive industry report
- An investment memo or market analysis
- A technical paper

### What We're NOT Claiming:
**1. We don't know exactly where economic value will come from first.**
Drop-in labor replacement? Construction? Warehouses? Eldercare? We don't know. The initial value will likely be worker replacement in specific verticals, not new use cases (those take time).

**2. Traditional robotics still matters.**
Factory arms, warehouse bots, specialized automation — these won't disappear. They'll continue driving value just as traditional software drives value alongside AI. Foundation model robotics is additive, not replacing everything.

**3. We're not making precise economic predictions.**
We're identifying what people are MISSING: the centralization toward cloud, the speed of capability transfer, the security motivations. The upside is significant. But we're not claiming to know P&L impacts.

### Why This Decision:
The "AI 2027" approach of concrete narrative timelines creates **hostages to fortune** — specific predictions that can be falsified by minor timing variations without disproving the core thesis. We're already in Q4 2025; many events I might have predicted for "coming months" have already occurred or haven't. Better to provide a structural argument and falsifiability criteria than to pretend we know the exact sequence.

### PART II: THE ARCHITECTURE (Sections 5-9)
*The slider is moving further than you think*

**Section 5: Introducing VIKI (For Those Who Haven't Seen i,Robot)**
In the 2004 film i,Robot, the villain isn't a rogue robot — it's VIKI, a central AI that controls all the robots through persistent network connection. Each NS-5 robot is a "dumb terminal" with a fast link to a single superintelligence. When VIKI decides humanity needs to be controlled "for its own good," every robot becomes an extension of that single will.

This seemed like dystopian sci-fi. It's actually the economically rational architecture.

Why VIKI makes sense:
- One brain, trained once, serves millions of bodies
- Every robot's experience improves the central model
- Overnight updates = instant fleet-wide improvement
- Hardware becomes commodity; intelligence is the moat
- This is exactly how ChatGPT works — your phone is a terminal to a datacenter brain

**Section 6: The Slider Argument**
The discourse treats cloud vs. edge as binary. It's actually a slider:
- **Pure edge:** All compute on-device (Tesla FSD approach, traditional robotics)
- **Hybrid:** Edge for control, cloud for mapping/planning (Waymo today)
- **Cloud-primary:** Edge for reflexes only, cloud for ALL reasoning (where this is heading)

What people miss: Even Waymo, the "on-device" poster child, uses cloud for mapping, route optimization, and fleet coordination. The question was never IF cloud would be involved — it's HOW MUCH.

Our argument: The slider is moving much further toward cloud than robotics experts expect. Figure Helix already shows this: 80M parameter S1 on-device for 200Hz reflexes, 7B parameter S2 for reasoning at 7Hz. But 7B is just the beginning — frontier reasoning requires 1T+ parameters, which ONLY works in cloud.

**Section 7: The Batch Economics**
- Single robot on-device: 10-30% GPU utilization
- Cloud datacenter: 60-80% GPU utilization via batching
- "The same GPU that runs one robot on-device can run 100+ robots in cloud"
- Inference cost declining 79%/year → cloud advantage compounds
- Model weight sharing: one copy of 1T parameters serves entire fleet simultaneously
- NOTE: Unlike text LLMs, VLAs can't share KV cache across robots (each sees different visual input) — the advantage is purely batch processing and amortized model weights

**Section 8: The Revenue Hunt (Why AI Labs Will Enter Robotics)**
AI labs are desperately searching for revenue beyond chat:
- ChatGPT: $2B ARR but $5B+ costs
- Enterprise SaaS is slow and competitive
- Consumer subscriptions have ceiling
- Coding assistants getting commoditized

Robotics is the obvious next frontier:
- Real-world value creation (not just information work)
- Clear ROI for customers (labor replacement)
- Recurring revenue (API calls per robot per hour)
- Massive TAM (85M labor shortage by 2030)

OpenAI is trying devices (phones, wearables). But the fundamental device for AGI to drive value in the physical world might not be a phone — it might be a humanoid. A phone can tell you how to fold laundry. A humanoid can fold it.

**Section 9: The Wintel Analogy (and the Apple Exception)**
- 1990s: PC OEMs (Dell, HP, IBM) commoditized; Microsoft + Intel captured >100% of industry profits
- BUT: Apple later proved integrated product companies can capture value through design + ecosystem
- Two winning positions in robotics:
  1. **The VIKI position** — cloud AI provider (like Microsoft/Intel)
  2. **The Apple position** — integrated product company using commodity manufacturing
- What DOESN'T work: being the hardware manufacturer without the intelligence layer (Foxconn, not Apple)
- Figure AI ended OpenAI collaboration — betting on Apple model, not terminal model
- Tesla, 1X also pursuing integrated approach
- The question isn't "hardware vs. software" but "who controls the intelligence layer"

### PART III: THE HARDWARE FLOOD (Sections 10-14)
*Why humanoids may scale faster than EVs did*

**Section 10: The Unitree Trajectory**

- 93% cost reduction in 2 years — FASTER than Moore's Law

- Key question: Is this "China Speed" or a one-time adjustment?

**Section 11: The EV Precedent (And Why Humanoids Are Easier)**
The EV ramp (2014-2024) showed what "China Speed" looks like: 75k units → 10M units in 10 years (63% CAGR). But humanoids have structural advantages:

| Factor | EVs (2014) | Humanoids (2025) | Implication |
|--------|-----------|------------------|-------------|
| Supply chain | Had to BUILD gigafactories | Already EXISTS (same components, smaller) | 5-year head start |
| Battery per unit | $15,000+ (60-100 kWh) | $500-1,500 (1-3 kWh) | 10-30x less capital |
| Parts count | 10,000+ | ~3,000 | Simpler assembly |
| Crash safety | Heavy regulation | Minimal (no passengers) | Faster iteration |
| Assembly time | 20-30 hours | 5-10 hours | Higher throughput |

The EV supply chain IS the humanoid supply chain. Same battery cells (smaller). Same motors (smaller). Same sensors. Same factories can adapt.

**Section 12: The Components Collapse**
- LIDAR: $75,000 (2012) → $100 (2025)
- Servo motors: commodity (drone/EV supply chain)
- IMUs: smartphone commodity
- Batteries: EV-driven cost curves, 1/30th the capacity needed
- Cameras: phone supply chain
- Compute: Jetson Orin ($999) can run edge inference

Key insight: Hardware manufacturing is increasingly solved. The bottleneck is intelligence — which is why this is an AI story, not a robotics story.

**Section 13: How Many Robots? (The Volume Question)**

Goldman Sachs (Feb 2025) projects 76,000 units by 2027, 250,000 by 2030. But they're modeling humanoids as industrial equipment with linear adoption curves.

If you model humanoids as the next EV — a state-mobilized industry parasitizing existing supply chains — the numbers look different:

| Scenario | 2027 | 2030 | Key Assumption |
|----------|------|------|----------------|
| A: Linear (Goldman) | 45k | 250k | Commercial demand only |
| B: EV Analogy | 100k | 800k | China push = EV push |
| C: Faster Than EV | 200k | 2.2M | Pre-built supply chain + simpler product |
| D: Security Mobilization | 350k | 4M | Strategic asset, dual US/China race |

Why might C or D be right?
- Supply chain already exists (EVs built it)
- Lower capex per unit ($6k vs $30k EV)
- Simpler product (no crash safety, fewer parts)
- Security motivation stronger than economic motivation was for EVs

**Section 14: The Security Multiplier**
The EV push was economic. The humanoid push is security.

Evidence of state mobilization:
- $140B Chinese government robotics fund
- PLA combat robot demonstrations (November 2025)
- PLA documents: robots for "completing the great cause of reunification"
- 470 robot density in China (vs. 162 global average)
- 100+ humanoid companies in China

When China mobilizes on security grounds (see: chips, EVs, solar), they don't do linear growth. They do OOM jumps.

The question isn't "will demand exist?" It's "how fast can supply scale when the state is pushing?"

Our base case: **100k-200k by 2027, 800k-2M by 2030**

This is 2-8x Goldman's estimate, justified by:
1. Pre-built supply chain (not in Goldman model)
2. Security motivation (not in Goldman model)
3. Faster cost decline than expected (Unitree evidence)
4. VLA progress accelerating (not in Goldman's Feb 2025 model)

Implication: Hardware companies must either control intelligence (Apple model) or become commoditized manufacturers (Foxconn model). At 2M units/year, this is a real industry.

**Section 16b: Where Does The Value Come From? (Sidebar)**

We're agnostic on which vertical breaks out first. But the structural logic:

**It must be worker replacement, not new use cases.** New use cases take years to emerge (the internet existed long before e-commerce scaled). Initial humanoid value = replacing human labor in existing workflows.

**But not ALL worker replacement.** Traditional industrial robots already dominate:
- Fixed assembly lines
- Welding, painting
- High-volume, structured environments

**Humanoids compete where traditional robotics CAN'T:**
| Traditional Robotics | Humanoid Advantage |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Fixed position | Mobile |
| Single task | Multi-task |
| Structured environment | Unstructured |
| High volume, low mix | Low volume, high mix |
| Purpose-built space | Human-designed space |

**The likeliest early verticals:**
1. **Warehousing** — Already happening (Amazon/Agility). Controlled environment, clear ROI.
2. **Manufacturing "last mile"** — Tasks that CAN'T be automated by fixed robots. Material handling, flexible assembly.
3. **Construction** — The sleeper. $1.4T US market, 500k unfilled jobs, tasks are actually repetitive (hanging drywall = same motion thousands of times), and China is building constantly.

**The traditional software analogy:** Excel didn't replace accountants — it made them 10x productive. Humanoids won't replace all factory workers. Traditional automation handles most of that. Humanoids fill the gaps where flexibility matters more than speed.

**What we're NOT predicting:** Exact TAM by vertical, which company wins, when consumer breaks out. Just: the structural forces are aligned, the capability is arriving, and someone will figure out the go-to-market.
